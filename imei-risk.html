IMEI Spoofing Risk

Unauthorized access using cloned IMEI numbers

üßæ Problem Description

An attacker who gains access to a corporate or employee-assigned mobile device's:

  • IMEI number (the unique identifier of the device)
  • and/or SIM card data (especially phone number and authentication details)

can connect to the mobile network pretending to be that device.

1. IMEI Spoofing

  • The attacker makes another device appear as if it has the same IMEI as the victim‚Äôs device.
  • On some rooted Android phones, IMEI can be changed manually.
  • As a result, the mobile carrier considers the attacker's device as legitimate and authorized.

2. SIM Swap Attack

  • The attacker collects the victim's personal data (e.g., national ID, date of birth).
  • They call the carrier pretending to be the victim and claim their SIM card is lost.
  • If identity checks are passed, the mobile number is transferred to a new SIM.
  • OTP (SMS verification), calls, and mobile access are now fully redirected to the attacker.
üí• Combined Threat of IMEI + SIM
  • Impersonates the victim‚Äôs number and device.
  • Can sniff unencrypted mobile traffic (e.g., DNS, HTTP).
  • May gain access to corporate systems that rely on IP/SMS-based verification.
  • Can monitor or exfiltrate sensitive data silently.
üß™ Real-World Corporate Scenario

An employee uses a corporate mobile device for email, VPN, and internal apps.

The attacker acquires the IMEI and SIM information and impersonates the user on the mobile network.

  • Accesses corporate VPN.
  • Receives OTPs for two-factor authentication.
  • Passes phone-based security checks.

üö® The employee may think they lost signal, but the attacker is already active on the network.

üîÑ Relation to MAC Spoofing and Corporate Network Risks

While IMEI and SIM spoofing apply to mobile networks, they share conceptual similarity with MAC spoofing in wired and wireless LANs.

  • IMEI identifies a mobile device; MAC address identifies a network device (wired/wireless).
  • In MAC spoofing, an attacker forges a trusted device‚Äôs MAC address to gain access.
  • This enables unauthorized entry and traffic interception.
üéØ Enterprise Risk

If an employee accidentally leaks their MAC address (via screenshot, email, or QR-based Wi-Fi), attackers could:

  • Clone the MAC to impersonate the device inside the company‚Äôs network.
  • Access internal VLANs or routing segments.
  • Bypass device whitelisting or firewall-based filters.
üõë MAC addresses must be treated as confidential in enterprise environments.

Since Cisco Packet Tracer does not simulate mobile networks, we cannot demonstrate IMEI or SIM spoofing directly.

Instead, we simulate a local attack using MAC spoofing, which reflects similar threat vectors:

  • Forged device identity
  • User/device impersonation
  • Internal network infiltration via cloned identifiers
